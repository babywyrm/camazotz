Threat Map Redesign — Three Approaches

Each solves the deep-link bug, progress model, and visual identity differently. Click to select your preference.

A

Hex Territory Map

Each lab is a hexagonal tile arranged in a honeycomb by threat category. Tiles glow/fill as you engage (flag captured = solid, walkthrough viewed = half-fill, untouched = dim). Clicking a tile opens a flyout with challenge link, walkthrough deep-link, and progress state. Categories form visual "regions" with color-coded borders.

Pros

  • Actually looks like a map / territory
  • Engagement is visible at a glance (glow states)
  • Striking, memorable, screenshot-worthy

Cons

  • More complex CSS/JS (hex grid layout)
  • Harder to scale if labs grow past ~30
  • Mobile layout needs careful handling
B

Radar / Coverage Chart

A radar/spider chart at the top shows coverage across 7 threat categories (arms of the radar). Below it, a refined card grid grouped by category. Each card tracks multi-level progress (flag, walkthrough, playground use). The radar fills in as you engage — your "threat coverage profile" emerges over time.

Pros

  • Radar chart is instantly readable as "coverage"
  • Cards below provide detail when needed
  • Familiar pattern (skills radar, security posture)

Cons

  • Radar chart needs SVG or canvas (more JS)
  • Still has cards — may feel like "grid with a chart on top"
  • Radar with 7 arms can look sparse
C

Attack Surface Dashboard

Rename from "Threat Map" to "Attack Surface." Top section: 7 category progress bars (not a chart — clean horizontal bars showing engagement %). Each bar expands into its labs on click. Labs show a 3-state progress indicator: ○ untouched → ◐ walkthrough viewed → ● flag captured. Deep-links work properly. Compact, professional, data-dense.

Pros

  • Simplest to build — CSS only, no SVG/canvas
  • Clean, professional, scales to any lab count
  • Collapsible categories keep it scannable
  • 3-state progress is richer than binary

Cons

  • Less visually striking than hex or radar
  • Doesn't "feel" like a map
  • May look too much like a settings page

All three approaches share: fixed deep-links (/operator#walkthrough/auth_lab → auto-enters that lab), multi-state progress tracking (untouched → walkthrough viewed → flag captured), and localStorage-based state with reset.
